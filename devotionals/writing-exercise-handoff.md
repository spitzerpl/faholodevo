# Writing Exercise Handoff — Prose Improvement Session

## Who I Am
I am a devotional writer working on a series called "Feasting on FAHOLO" (Faith, Hope, Love). I have completed four devotionals so far based on Romans 5:1-10, Romans 12:1-13, 1 Corinthians 13:1-14:1, and Galatians 5:5-6. I am 48 years old and committed to improving my craft.

## My Writing Profile

### Strengths
- Strong theological architecture — I build clear structural frameworks for passages (e.g., identifying "two hopes" in a text, reframing the Romans Road as leading TO Romans 12)
- Ambitious cross-referencing across Scripture
- Good instinct for memorable phrases when I land them ("Love frees us to start the journey, walk the journey, and finish the journey of faith")
- Strong practical application — concrete, specific, not generic
- Personality in my voice — humor (Eutychus joke, infomercial analogy), relatable analogies (health kick, treasure map), direct address

### Weakness to Work On: Sentence Structure (Comma Splices and Run-On Sentences)
I consistently join independent clauses with commas or insufficient punctuation where a period, semicolon, or restructured sentence is needed. This causes my theological precision to get muddied by grammatical ambiguity — the reader has to untangle which ideas belong together and which are separate claims.

Examples from my actual work:

1. "There is nothing more counterfeited today than love, however our love is to be genuine" — two independent clauses joined by a comma. Needs a semicolon before "however" or a period.

2. "It is about obeying the truth, wherever it leads (7). And where it leads to is no longer a mystery, for it leads to..." — "for" is doing conjunctive work but the sentence has already made its point; a new sentence would let the conclusion land harder.

3. "If you've lost your way, and you're life has become more than these (which generally means it stopped being these) what do you do?" — a parenthetical aside embedded in a comma-spliced conditional that runs long enough to lose the question it's building toward.

4. "Christ is our hope of glory (Col 1:27) and we rejoice because we know he is not leading us to an empty chest" — two independent clauses with no conjunction separation.

The pattern: my theological arguments are built on precise distinctions (two kinds of hope, love as foundation vs. product, faith's value from its object). When the grammar blurs where one idea ends and the next begins, those distinctions lose their punch.

## The Practice Passage: Psalm 73:1-28 (ESV)

A Psalm of Asaph.

1 Truly God is good to Israel, to those who are pure in heart.
2 But as for me, my feet had almost stumbled, my steps had nearly slipped.
3 For I was envious of the arrogant when I saw the prosperity of the wicked.
4 For they have no pangs until death; their bodies are fat and sleek.
5 They are not in trouble as others are; they are not stricken like the rest of mankind.
6 Therefore pride is their necklace; violence covers them as a garment.
7 Their eyes swell out through fatness; their hearts overflow with follies.
8 They scoff and speak with malice; loftily they threaten oppression.
9 They set their mouths against the heavens, and their tongue struts through the earth.
10 Therefore his people turn back to them, and find no fault in them.
11 And they say, "How can God know? Is there knowledge in the Most High?"
12 Behold, these are the wicked; always at ease, they increase in riches.
13 All in vain have I kept my heart clean and washed my hands in innocence.
14 For all the day long I have been stricken and rebuked every morning.
15 If I had said, "I will speak thus," I would have betrayed the generation of your children.
16 But when I thought how to understand this, it seemed to me a wearisome task,
17 until I went into the sanctuary of God; then I discerned their end.
18 Truly you set them in slippery places; you make them fall to ruin.
19 How they are destroyed in a moment, swept away utterly by terrors!
20 Like a dream when one awakes, O Lord, when you rouse yourself, you despise them as phantoms.
21 When my soul was embittered, when I was pricked in heart,
22 I was brutish and ignorant; I was like a beast toward you.
23 Nevertheless, I am continually with you; you hold my right hand.
24 You guide me with your counsel, and afterward you will receive me to glory.
25 Whom have I in heaven but you? And there is nothing on earth that I desire besides you.
26 My flesh and my heart may fail, but God is the strength of my heart and my portion forever.
27 For behold, those who are far from you shall perish; you put an end to everyone who is unfaithful to you.
28 But for me it is good to be near God; I have made the Lord GOD my refuge, that I may tell of all your works.

## Why This Passage
Psalm 73 has a natural emotional arc — envy, doubt, turning point in the sanctuary, resolution in trust. Writing about it will naturally produce sentences with multiple moving parts (because the psalmist's journey has layers). This makes it an ideal exercise for practicing clean clause boundaries while handling complex theological and emotional content.
